History & Discovery

The Birth of a Legend

In the second half of 1992, a young Sony Music Entertainment producer named Kazunori Yamauchi began sketching a dream: a racing simulator so faithful to real physics that players could feel the weight of a car shifting through a corner. Most industry veterans said it was impossible on a home console. Yamauchi pitched the idea to Sony and was immediately rejected. A realistic driving game was seen as commercially unviable — racing games at the time were all speed and spectacle, with physics purely in service of fun, not fidelity.

The turning point came after the unexpected success of Sony's cartoon racers Motor Toon Grand Prix (1994) and its sequel — games that Yamauchi himself had produced. They proved his team could ship. He pitched Gran Turismo again. This time, Sony's business planner Shuhei Yoshida helped push it through. The project was greenlit.

The Smallest Team for the Biggest Game
At its largest, the Gran Turismo development team numbered just fifteen people. For much of the five-year development, it operated with seven to eleven developers — quietly, and mostly in secret within Sony. The result would become the best-selling PlayStation game of all time.

Development ran from 1992 to late 1997 on a budget of roughly $5 million — a figure that alarmed Sony executives who expected games in one to two years. Gran Turismo launched in Japan in December 1997, reaching North America and Europe by mid-1998. In a final piece of historical irony: the studio responsible for it wasn't even formally named yet when it shipped. Polyphony Digital was only established in April 1998 — five months after the Japanese launch.

The Shuhei Yoshida Connection
The young business planner who helped champion Gran Turismo's second pitch inside Sony was Shuhei Yoshida — later the President of Sony Computer Entertainment Worldwide Studios, and one of PlayStation's most recognisable public figures for decades. He was a producer on the original game. Gran Turismo launched two careers simultaneously: Yamauchi's and his.

How It Works

Your Licence, Please

Gran Turismo's structure was unlike any racing game that had come before. Before you could enter most events, you had to earn the right to drive. A series of timed driving tests — Licence Tests — assessed your competence at specific techniques before gating you into progressively faster championships. It wasn't just clever game design; for many 1997 players, it was their first exposure to concepts like braking zones, trail braking, and managing understeer through a fast corner.

Simulation Mode began with a meagre 10,000 credits and a used-car lot. The gap between what you could afford and what you wanted was the whole game. You'd buy a second-hand Honda Civic, grind the Sunday Cup, watch credits accumulate, bolt on a sports exhaust, and slowly — circuit by circuit — work toward cars you'd only ever seen in magazines. How It Works

Simulating Steel

The heart of Gran Turismo's revolution wasn't the number of cars, or the glossy replays, or even the licence tests. It was the physics. For the first time on a home console, you could feel a car thinking. A rear-wheel-drive coupe would swap ends if you lifted off mid-corner. A front-heavy hatchback would understeer stubbornly into the barrier if you carried too much speed. Tyres lost grip progressively rather than snapping instantly to a spin.

Yamauchi's team built on the physics model from Motor Toon Grand Prix — the same programmer, the same underlying algorithms, but pushed into an entirely different register. They simulated effects that had never been modelled on consumer hardware: tyre deformation across a slip angle curve, chassis weight transfer under braking and acceleration, and suspension response over kerbs.

⚖️

Weight Transfer

Braking shifts mass forward, acceleration shifts it back, cornering loads the outside wheels. GT modelled this continuously — the car you drove at corner entry was physically different from the car at the apex.

📐

Slip Angle

Every tyre has a slip angle: the difference between the direction it points and the direction it travels. Too much or too little and grip collapses — GT was one of the first games to simulate this curve realistically.

🔄

Understeer & Oversteer

Front-wheel-drive cars pushed wide. Rear-wheel-drive cars slid the tail. Each car had a distinct, modelled character that demanded different inputs — and rewarded drivers who adapted.

🛞

Suspension Response

Kerbs and bumps transmitted shock through the chassis, momentarily unloading tyres at exactly the wrong instant. Ride height and spring stiffness — adjustable in the garage — directly changed this behaviour.

This wasn't just impressive — it meant you could get genuinely better at Gran Turismo by understanding real physics. Players who learned to brake later and carry more corner speed became faster. The game was quietly teaching mechanics that transfer to a real car on a real track. Contemporary reviewers struggled to articulate it. IGN called it "the best racing game to date." Edge described it as "rare indeed, almost nonexistent" that a racing game could appeal simultaneously to arcade fans and simulation purists. Neither review was exaggerating.

The Deep Dive

The Garage

Gran Turismo launched with around 140 licensed cars — a number that would have seemed absurd to the racing game developers of 1995. More importantly, they were real vehicles with real performance data, not approximations. And for Western audiences, a large portion of the roster was genuinely unknown: Japanese domestic market machinery that had never been officially exported, whispered about in import car magazines but never experienced. Gran Turismo didn't just include these cars — it introduced them to a global generation.

Mazda MX-5 (Miata) NA

1989 · Rear-wheel drive · 1.6L inline-4 · Naturally aspirated

One of the finest starter cars in GT history — not because it was fast, but because it was honest. The MX-5 was tail-happy enough to teach you about rear-wheel-drive balance, light enough to feel every input, and cheap enough to buy early. Slide it wide, correct it, feel the rear step out and gather again. No other cheap car in the game taught you more. Many players look back on their first MX-5 corner as the moment Gran Turismo clicked.

Nissan Skyline GT-R V·Spec (R33)

1995 · All-wheel drive · 2.6L RB26DETT twin-turbo

The GT-R was the aspirational machine — the car everyone saved for. Its ATTESA E-TS all-wheel-drive system sent torque to the front only when the rears lost grip, giving it almost supernatural composure at speed. In the game, a tuned R33 GT-R was capable of winning almost any race. It was fast. It was stable. It handled in a way that felt slightly unfair compared to ordinary rear-wheel-drive machines. And for Western players who had never heard of a Skyline, it was a revelation. Japan had been building this, and nobody told us.

Mazda RX-7 FD3S

1992 · Rear-wheel drive · 1.3L 13B-REW twin-rotor Wankel · Twin-turbo sequential

The RX-7 FD was genuinely exotic — not just for its looks but for what sat under the bonnet. A twin-rotor Wankel engine has no pistons, almost no reciprocating mass, and revs with a smoothness and willingness that no conventional piston engine can match. Its sequential twin-turbo setup added a distinctive urgency to the power delivery: calm below 4,500 rpm, then a surge as the second turbine spun up. In Gran Turismo, the RX-7 was a late-game monster — light, powerful, and wickedly agile. For many players, it was their first encounter with the rotary engine concept, and they never forgot it.

The Circuits

The Circuits

Gran Turismo's tracks were almost entirely fictional — invented circuits with their own personalities, histories, and moods. And yet they felt completely credible. Each layout had an internal logic, a story you could read in the corners: Trial Mountain's blind crests and tunnel sections described exactly what kind of mountain road it was. Grand Valley's long, flowing sweepers had the feel of a purpose-built Grand Prix venue with real heritage. These were not random courses — they were places.

Several of these circuits have appeared in every Gran Turismo game made since 1997, a continuity now spanning almost three decades. They are, in a real sense, sacred ground for the community.

Cultural Context

Moon Over The Castle

Gran Turismo didn't just sound good — it sounded surprising. Yamauchi's team made an unusual choice for a racing game: jazz fusion. Not smooth background music, but genuinely energetic, technically accomplished jazz with complex harmonics and driving rhythms. It was an unexpected fit that somehow worked perfectly — sophisticated, confident, and completely unlike the guitar riffs and drum machines players expected from action games.

The soundtrack for the Japanese version was composed primarily by Masahiro Andoh, guitarist and bandleader of T-Square — one of Japan's most celebrated fusion ensembles, with decades of recordings behind them — alongside composer Isamu Ohira. The result was unlike anything else in gaming at the time: polished, sophisticated, and oddly appropriate for sitting in a garage at midnight tuning a Skyline.

Japan

Original Soundtrack

Jazz fusion from Masahiro Andoh and T-Square, with an original score built around complex harmonic structures and live instrumentation. The opening theme — Moon Over The Castle — became the series' eternal signature, an orchestral and jazz piece of quiet grandeur that has appeared in every mainline Gran Turismo title ever made.

North America & Europe

Western Soundtrack

Sony Computer Entertainment Europe's Jason Page composed a completely different score: harder, more industrial electronic music — more conventionally "video game." For millions of Western players, this was Gran Turismo's sound. They never heard the jazz version at all, and experienced an entirely different emotional atmosphere as a result.

Two Games in One Room
A Japanese player and a British player who both loved Gran Turismo in 1997 were, in a meaningful sense, playing games with different souls. The car physics were identical. The circuits were identical. But the music shaped the atmosphere so completely that the emotional experience was entirely different. Many Western players who first heard Moon Over The Castle in a later entry describe it as a disorienting but beautiful discovery — as if the game they knew had a secret history they'd never been told about.

A physical album, The Sound of Gran Turismo, was released on EMI Records in November 1998 — an extraordinary publishing move for a video game soundtrack in that era, and a signal of how seriously Yamauchi's team regarded music as a creative pillar of the experience, not just background noise.

Scale & Impact

Legacy & Numbers

Gran Turismo sold 10.85 million copies on the original PlayStation — more than any other game on the platform. It outsold every Mario, every Final Fantasy, and every Crash Bandicoot title in the system's library. It went on to anchor a franchise that, across eight mainline entries, has sold over 90 million copies — one of the most successful racing game series ever made, still active and still critically relevant in 2024.

Beyond sales, Gran Turismo changed what the industry expected from racing games. It established a template — deep garage, licence system, tuning, cinematic replays — that dozens of competitors would spend the next decade trying to replicate. It showed definitively that simulation and accessibility weren't opposites: you could have real physics and still sell ten million copies. And it launched an entire JDM car culture among Western audiences who discovered their love of Evolutions and Skylines through a PlayStation controller.

The eighth mainline title returns to the single-player GT Mode structure of the original. Moon Over The Castle plays over the opening cinematic — as it has done in every main GT game since 1997.

From Controller to Cockpit
Nissan's GT Academy programme (2008–2016) took the game's feedback loop to its logical conclusion: real racing licenses awarded to Gran Turismo players who could demonstrate real skill. Several graduates competed professionally in endurance racing — including Le Mans. Gran Turismo had gone full circle: a simulator used to teach real driving skills, producing drivers who won real races.

Surprising Details

Pit Stop: Things You Might Not Know

🚗

No Driving Licence Required

Kazunori Yamauchi reportedly did not hold a driving licence when he began developing Gran Turismo in 1992. His love of cars was for them as machines and objects of engineering — not necessarily as something he drove to work.

🏭

Named After the Game Shipped

Polyphony Digital was only formally established in April 1998 — five months after Gran Turismo launched in Japan. The studio that made one of the greatest PlayStation games of all time didn't have its iconic name when it shipped.

🎮

The Secret Inside Motor Toon

The physics engine powering Motor Toon Grand Prix's unusually competent handling was quietly being refined for Gran Turismo all along. The cartoonish racer was, in part, a live testbed for the simulation Yamauchi was building in parallel.

🌍

Two Completely Different Games

Beyond the music, the Japanese and Western versions had different car rosters — Japan's release included more JDM-exclusive models not available in the Western version. Players in different territories were genuinely experiencing different garages.

🎵

A Theme That Never Left

Moon Over The Castle has featured in every mainline Gran Turismo game from 1997 through GT7 in 2022 — a 25-year musical presence in one franchise. It is one of the longest-running leitmotifs in gaming history.

💸

The Budget That Alarmed Sony

$5 million over five years was considered excessive and borderline reckless by Sony executives — games were not supposed to take that long or cost that much in 1997. It became one of the most profitable pieces of software in PlayStation's history.
