Overview
A family SUV with no business going this fast

The Subaru Forester STI is a car that shouldn't exist in any rational product lineup. In the early 2000s, Subaru's STI division — responsible for the brand's rally-winning, tarmac-terrorising performance cars — turned their attention not to a hot hatch or sports car, but to the boxy, practical Forester. The result was a four-wheel-drive family hauler that shared its beating heart with the Impreza WRX STI.

In Japan, the Forester STI (internally coded SG9) launched in 2004 and sold exclusively through Subaru's domestic STI dealer network. It was never officially offered in Europe, the United States, or Australia — though many have since been grey-imported into those markets. The pitch was simple: all the capability and thrills of an STI, but with enough room for child seats, a dog, and a full boot of camping gear.

Why JDM only? Subaru's global lineup already had the Impreza WRX STI in markets like the UK and Australia. Adding a more expensive, more powerful Forester STI would have cannibalized sales and muddied the brand positioning. Japan's domestic market, with its appetite for niche performance variants, was the perfect and only home.

What made it genuinely special wasn't just the engine transplant. STI's engineers overhauled the suspension, brakes, transmission, and chassis to handle the power in a car with a higher centre of gravity than the Impreza. The result drove nothing like a standard Forester — it drove like a tall, all-weather, impossibly practical WRX STI.

History & Discovery
How it came to exist

By the early 2000s, Subaru Tecnica International had established a clear formula: take a standard Subaru, fit the high-output turbocharged flat-four, add a close-ratio six-speed gearbox, STI-tuned suspension, and sell it to serious enthusiasts. That formula had produced world rally champions. Applying it to the Forester was a logical — if unexpected — extension.

The Forester STI's creation reflected something important about Subaru's culture at the time. STI was not simply a badge-engineering department — it was staffed by engineers who genuinely cared about chassis dynamics, and the brief to build a performance Forester was taken seriously at every level of development.

Deep Dive
The EJ207: Subaru's crown jewel

The engine in the Forester STI is not some detuned or slightly warmed-over version of a regular turbocharged flat-four. It is the EJ207 — the same unit found in the Impreza WRX STI, the legendary 22B special edition, and the Prodrive-prepared World Rally Championship cars. Fitting this engine into the Forester was not a compromise; it was a genuine parts-bin raid at the very top of Subaru's catalogue.

The EJ207 displaces 2.0 litres, arranged in a horizontally opposed "boxer" configuration — four cylinders lying flat, two on each side, firing across each other. This layout keeps the engine's mass low in the chassis, contributing to a lower centre of gravity than any upright four-cylinder could achieve. The crankshaft sits below the bonnet line, a packaging advantage that is baked into Subaru's DNA and directly inherited from their aviation engine heritage.

The Gentleman's Agreement cap. Japanese manufacturers in this era adhered to a voluntary limit of 280 ps (276 bhp / 206 kW) for domestic market cars — a self-imposed ceiling agreed between manufacturers to avoid a horsepower war. The EJ207 in the Forester STI is rated at exactly 280 ps on paper. In practice, these engines consistently measure higher on dynos. Standard, unmodified examples regularly show 300–320 bhp at the wheels.

Key to the EJ207's character is its twin-scroll turbocharger. Unlike a single-scroll unit, the twin-scroll design separates exhaust pulses from pairs of cylinders into distinct channels within the turbine housing. This reduces turbo lag significantly and improves spool-up, giving the engine a more linear power delivery than the stereotypical turbo rush. Paired with Subaru's intercooler and STI's revised ECU mapping, the result is an engine that feels eager from low revs and pulls hard through to its 7,000 rpm redline.

The block itself is a cast-iron unit — unusual in an era when aluminium was becoming standard — chosen for its rigidity and ability to handle the stresses of forced induction. STI-specific pistons and connecting rods are forged rather than cast, making them substantially stronger under load. The cylinder head features sodium-filled exhaust valves to manage heat, a detail borrowed directly from racing engine practice.

This is an engine that rewards proper maintenance and punishes neglect. Oil changes matter. The turbo seals are not forgiving of cold starts followed by immediate hard driving. Treat it correctly and 200,000 km is well within reach. Ignore it and the repair bills will be significant. This partly explains why pristine examples with documented service histories command such strong premiums today.

Under the skin
STI vs. standard Forester: what actually changed

Calling it badge-engineered is a misunderstanding. The list of changes from a standard Forester XT turbo to the STI is extensive — this is a comprehensively re-engineered car, not a sticker and spring job.

The DCCD advantage. The Driver's Control Centre Differential is the jewel in the crown. It allows the driver to bias torque distribution between the front and rear axles — from fully open (rotation-friendly, more playful) through to fully locked (maximum traction on loose or slippery surfaces). No standard Forester offered anything remotely close to this level of driver control.
Counterintuitive facts
Things that defy the obvious
It's faster than a 2004 Porsche Boxster S
The 2004 Boxster S ran 0–100 km/h in around 5.4 seconds. A boxy, tall, practical family SUV designed to carry camping gear beat it off the line. They were aimed at completely different buyers — but the numbers don't care about that.
The 280 ps figure is almost certainly wrong
The Japanese gentleman's agreement capped power at 280 ps on paper. Forester STIs regularly produce 300–320 bhp on independent dynamometer tests in completely stock, unmodified form. Subaru submitted the agreed figure and quietly built more engine than they admitted.
The iconic rear wing is entirely optional
The enormous multi-element rear wing that defines the look of many Forester STIs was a factory option — the "Sport Package" — not standard fitment. Buyers could choose a cleaner, wingless appearance. Both versions are genuine, complete STIs.
It was priced comparably to a Lancer Evolution
At launch in Japan, the Forester STI was priced similarly to a Mitsubishi Lancer Evolution VIII. Japanese domestic pricing made no attempt to extract a large premium over the Impreza STI. Grey import prices to other markets often doubled the cost.
The burble is a mathematical accident
The signature Subaru idle note — that loping, uneven burble — is a byproduct of the EJ-series engine's odd-fire crankshaft angle. Cylinders don't fire at perfectly equal intervals, creating an uneven pulse. Some tuners straighten it out; purists consider that blasphemy.
It retains a meaningful tow rating
Unlike most performance cars of the era, the Forester STI kept a 1,500 kg tow rating. You could theoretically tow a trailer to a track day, unhitch, and then embarrass sports cars in the paddock. Impractical. Magnificent. The option exists.
Limited Edition
The S401: Subaru's rarest Forester

In 2005, STI produced an extreme variant of the Forester STI called the S401 — named for its production limit of exactly 400 numbered units. Where the standard Forester STI already pushed the boundaries of what a family SUV should do, the S401 went further still.

Each S401 was individually built with additional components hand-fitted by STI technicians. The result was a car that felt closer to a motorsport-adjacent touring vehicle than anything with rear seats and a full boot had any right to feel.

400
Units produced — each individually numbered

The S401 received a revised ECU pushing power beyond the standard 280 ps ceiling, STI-specification Bilstein dampers, a strut tower brace, a revised front lip spoiler, and 18-inch BBS forged wheels. Interior changes included a numbered identification plaque and upgraded STI Recaro seats with additional side bolstering.

Finding an S401 today requires patience, budget, and some luck. Many have been modified, which reduces collector value. The small production run means supply is genuinely constrained — not artificially inflated scarcity. Numbers-matching, original, unmodified S401s with full service histories are now considered the holy grail of Forester collecting.

Legacy & Value
Why collectors pay attention now

When the Forester STI was new, it was a niche product appreciated by enthusiasts who needed practicality but wanted performance. Two decades later it has become something else: a collectible JDM classic whose values have risen consistently as the performance SUV genre it pioneered has become the automotive mainstream.

The cars that now fill the Forester STI's spiritual role — the Volkswagen Tiguan R, BMW X3 M, Alfa Romeo Stelvio Quadrifoglio — are larger, heavier, more expensive, and far more electronically complex. None of them have the raw mechanical simplicity, the flat-four burble, or the Japan-only mystique of the SG9.

What happened to values? A low-mileage Forester STI that sold for roughly ¥3.5 million new in Japan now changes hands for substantially more when grey-imported to the UK or Australia. The S401 in particular has seen dramatic appreciation, with clean examples trading for two to three times the original retail price in enthusiast markets.

The irony is that the Forester STI's practicality — the very quality that once made it seem less exotic than a pure sports car — is now part of its appeal. You can use it as your only car. You can road trip in it. You can service it at any Subaru specialist. And then you can find a mountain road and remind a local sports car owner what a naturally-aspirated-soul AWD weapon feels like when it's working properly.

Subaru has not built a Forester with this specification since 2007. Given the brand's trajectory toward CVTs and PHEV platforms, it is unlikely they ever will again. That makes every surviving SG9 Forester STI — and every S401 in particular — genuinely irreplaceable.
